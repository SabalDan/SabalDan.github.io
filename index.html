About

Where Field Botany
Meets Genomic Science

I am a doctoral candidate in Earth Systems Science at Florida International University, where I work in the Conservation & Sustainable Horticulture Laboratory under Dr. Amir Ali Khoddamzadeh. My research sits at the intersection of conservation biology, ecophysiology, and genomics, with a focus on the endangered and taxonomically complex Sabal palm taxa of South Florida.

As Seedbank Coordinator and Montgomery Botanical Graduate Fellow at Montgomery Botanical Center, my day-to-day work centers on cycad pollinations, pollen cryobanking, and the distribution of germplasm to conservation partners — bridging living collections stewardship with the broader goals of ex situ conservation. I also conduct morphological studies of palms in the MBC living collection, work that runs in parallel with and directly informs my dissertation research.

A defining moment in my career was co-authoring the rediscovery of wild populations of Sabal miamiensis — a palm species listed as Extinct in the Wild since 1997. In 2024, field surveys I led with Montgomery Botanical Center confirmed a surviving population on protected land in Miami-Dade County, and subsequent work revealed a second population, bringing the known total to approximately 70–100 individuals. I have since led the first deliberate reintroduction of nursery-propagated plants into the wild.

Research Focus

How I Work to Save
Endangered Palms

🧬

Conservation Genomics

By sequencing the DNA of wild and garden-grown Sabal palms across South Florida, I map genetic diversity within and between populations, identify which lineages are most distinct, and inform decisions about what to collect, cross, and reintroduce. A reference genome assembly for S. miamiensis — the first for the genus — is currently underway via PacBio HiFi long-read sequencing.

🌿

Integrative Taxonomy

Before you can protect a species, you have to know it exists. I use a combination of morphometrics, flow cytometry, and molecular data to resolve species boundaries within the South Florida Sabal complex. This approach led to co-authoring the rediscovery of wild S. miamiensis — listed as Extinct in the Wild since 1997 — and continues to reveal cryptic diversity in the region.

🌱

Ex Situ Conservation

When a species is down to a handful of individuals, every seed matters. At Montgomery Botanical Center, I coordinate hand-pollination of rare cycads and palms, develop pollen cryobanking and storage protocols, work on in vitro micropropagation, and move germplasm to partner institutions. These living collections form an insurance policy for species that may no longer survive in the wild on their own.

📡

Ecophysiology

Understanding how a palm responds to drought, flooding, and salt helps predict which populations will struggle most as South Florida's climate shifts. By measuring performance across environments — from seasonally flooded prairies to coastal hammocks — I can identify the most resilient individuals and prioritize them for conservation.

🗺️

Biogeography & GIS

Finding a rare palm starts long before setting foot in the field. Using satellite imagery, elevation data, and historical records, I build maps of where South Florida's threatened palms survive and where overlooked populations may remain. This GIS-based approach guided the discovery of both known wild S. miamiensis populations.

🌾

Conservation Horticulture

Seeds collected today become the wild populations of tomorrow. I develop propagation protocols and institutional partnerships that keep rare palm and cycad germplasm moving between botanical gardens and into the hands of restoration practitioners — working toward the goal of returning threatened species to the landscapes where they belong.

Focal Taxa

Species of Focus

Fieldwork spans the Everglades, pine rocklands, and coastal hammocks of South Florida — ecosystems defined by fire, hydrology, and calcareous geology. These are the palms at the center of my research program.

Sabal miamiensis

Miami Palm

A critically imperiled endemic of Miami-Dade County, described from the oolitic limestone outcrops of the Miami Rock Ridge and listed as Extinct in the Wild by IUCN in 1997. In 2024, field surveys I led with Montgomery Botanical Center confirmed a surviving wild population — the first verified occurrence in nearly 30 years. Continued surveys in 2025 revealed a second population, bringing the known total to approximately 70–100 palms. The central focus of my dissertation research.

Critically Imperiled

Sabal sp. nov.

Undescribed Species

A putative new species distinguished by genome size, morphology, and strict association with calcareous substrates in South Florida. Formal description integrating flow cytometry, morphometrics, and ecological niche data is in preparation. Species name withheld pending publication.

Description in Preparation

Sabal palmetto

Cabbage Palm — Florida State Tree

Florida's state tree and a keystone species of coastal and interior ecosystems. Distinctive South Florida populations — adapted to xeric scrub, coastal hammocks, and seasonally flooded prairies — are threatened by sea level rise and land use change, and serve as an important comparative reference within my research on the South Florida Sabal complex.

Native — Secure

Attalea crassispatha

Haitian Attalea

Among the world's rarest palms, with only a small number of individuals remaining in southwestern Haiti. At Montgomery Botanical Center, I am developing controlled pollination strategies and pollen storage solutions for accessions held in the living collection, contributing to ongoing ex situ conservation efforts for this critically endangered species.
